# The Gilded Bazaar

## 00.5 – World Design Philosophy & Lore Framework

**Version:** 1.0
**Status:** Living Document

---

# Purpose

This document defines the world-building philosophy of The Gilded Bazaar.

It establishes how worlds are modelled, generated and maintained without prescribing a single official setting.

The platform is designed to support countless unique campaigns while maintaining believable, internally consistent worlds.

This specification provides the conceptual foundation for:

* Living World Engine
* Merchant Engine
* Economy Engine
* AI Generation Systems
* NPC Systems
* Settlement Simulation
* Downtime
* World Events

---

# Philosophy

The Gilded Bazaar does **not** provide a canonical world.

Instead, it provides a framework capable of supporting any fantasy setting.

Every generated settlement, merchant, organisation or event should feel as though it belongs naturally within its campaign.

The platform should inspire creativity rather than replace it.

---

# The Living World Principle

The world should feel alive regardless of whether players are currently playing.

Time continues.

Trade continues.

Merchants travel.

Harvests succeed or fail.

Guilds rise and fall.

Rumours spread.

Political tensions evolve.

The players are important participants in the world—but they are not the only force acting upon it.

---

# The Rule of Internal Consistency

Generated content should always respect previously established world information.

Examples:

* A desert kingdom should not suddenly generate snowy forests.
* A low-magic village should not routinely sell legendary magical artefacts.
* A kingdom at war should reflect shortages, military activity and political uncertainty.
* A remote hamlet should not contain a bustling international marketplace unless there is a compelling narrative reason.

Consistency is more valuable than surprise.

---

# Campaign Ownership

Every campaign belongs to its Dungeon Master.

The platform provides suggestions.

The Dungeon Master decides what becomes canon.

AI-generated content is never automatically accepted as official world history.

# World Layers

Every campaign world is described through interconnected layers.

## Layer 1 — Geography

Examples:

* Continents
* Kingdoms
* Regions
* Cities
* Villages
* Wilderness
* Coastlines
* Rivers
* Mountains
* Roads

Geography influences every downstream system.

---

## Layer 2 — Climate

Examples:

* Temperate
* Desert
* Tropical
* Arctic
* Highland
* Coastal

Climate affects:

* Agriculture
* Clothing
* Trade
* Available resources
* Seasonal events
* Merchant stock

---

## Layer 3 — Civilisation

Each settlement should define:

* Population
* Wealth
* Education
* Security
* Government
* Infrastructure
* Magic acceptance
* Technology level

These values influence both AI generation and gameplay systems.

---

## Layer 4 — Economy

Economies should emerge naturally from geography and civilisation.

Examples:

* Mining towns export ore.
* Coastal settlements specialise in fishing and shipbuilding.
* Forest villages trade timber and game.
* Desert caravans transport luxury goods.

Economic identity should always make sense.

---

## Layer 5 — Culture

Every settlement should develop its own identity.

Examples:

* Festivals
* Traditions
* Cuisine
* Architecture
* Clothing
* Hospitality
* Superstitions
* Local customs

Culture creates memorable locations.

---

## Layer 6 — Religion & Belief

Religion influences more than temples.

It affects:

* Holidays
* Laws
* Architecture
* Festivals
* Social expectations
* Merchant availability
* Political alliances

The framework should support any pantheon or belief system.

---

## Layer 7 — Politics

Political systems should influence daily life.

Examples:

* Monarchy
* Republic
* City-state
* Merchant council
* Religious order
* Military occupation
* Tribal leadership

Political changes become world events.

---

## Layer 8 — Factions

Factions provide long-term continuity.

Examples:

* Merchant guilds
* Noble houses
* Criminal syndicates
* Adventuring companies
* Religious orders
* Academic institutions
* Mercenary companies

Factions should possess:

* Goals
* Reputation
* Resources
* Rivals
* Allies
* Territory

---

# Settlements

Every settlement should feel unique.

Core attributes include:

* Name
* Population
* Wealth
* Security
* Prosperity
* Government
* Dominant industries
* Trade connections
* Current events
* Notable NPCs
* Available merchants
* Local rumours

No two settlements should feel interchangeable.

---

# Merchants

Merchants exist as members of the world rather than inventory screens.

Every merchant has:

* A profession
* A home
* A schedule
* Relationships
* Opinions
* Financial motivations
* Inventory sources

The shop exists because the merchant exists.

---

# NPC Philosophy

NPCs should possess lives outside player interaction.

Each significant NPC should have:

* Personal goals
* Relationships
* Occupation
* Daily routine
* Reputation
* Secrets
* Motivations

NPCs should evolve alongside the campaign.

---

# Time

Time is a first-class system.

Support should include:

* Days
* Weeks
* Months
* Seasons
* Years
* Festivals
* Historical eras

Future rule systems may define their own calendars.

---

# History

Campaign history should accumulate automatically.

Examples:

* Important purchases
* Major quests
* World events
* Political changes
* Settlement growth
* Merchant bankruptcies
* Legendary discoveries

History gives the world memory.

---

# World Events

Events should influence multiple systems simultaneously.

Examples:

* Harvest Festival
* Royal Coronation
* Trade Caravan Arrival
* Drought
* Plague
* Mine Collapse
* Dragon Attack
* Religious Pilgrimage
* Civil War
* Arcane Anomaly

Events should have:

* Start date
* Duration
* Affected regions
* Economic effects
* NPC reactions
* Merchant adjustments
* Rumours
* Quest opportunities

---

# AI World Generation

AI should generate content through structured context.

Generation inputs should include:

* Existing world state
* Campaign themes
* Geography
* Current events
* Population
* Economy
* Faction activity
* Previous history

Outputs must extend the world rather than contradict it.

---

# Player Influence

Players should meaningfully shape the world.

Examples:

* Opening a successful business increases prosperity.
* Saving a settlement improves reputation.
* Destroying trade routes causes shortages.
* Funding temples increases religious influence.
* Supporting factions changes political balance.

The world should remember player actions.

---

# System Agnosticism

The framework should remain independent of any single tabletop RPG system.

Rules-specific content belongs in configurable data.

Core platform concepts—settlements, merchants, economies, inventories, factions and history—remain reusable across different roleplaying systems.

---

# World Generation Principles

Every generated element should satisfy four criteria:

1. **Believable** – It makes sense within the established world.
2. **Consistent** – It respects existing lore and previous events.
3. **Useful** – It creates opportunities for play rather than unnecessary detail.
4. **Expandable** – It provides hooks for future stories.

---

# Definition of Success

A campaign world should feel persistent.

Players should believe that the world continues to exist even while they are away from the table.

Every return should reveal subtle signs that time has passed and life has continued.
